What readiness means

Readiness isn't just about having the right software; it's about the state of your operational process. An AI automation readiness scorecard evaluates whether a workflow is clear, repeatable, and safe enough to be handed over to an agent. It measures the quality of your inputs, the consistency of your rules, and the level of risk involved if the automation makes a mistake.

The 0–30 model

We use a practical 0–30 scoring model to assess workflows. This model looks at 10 distinct categories, scoring each from 0 to 3. A low score indicates a process that is too chaotic or risky to automate, while a high score points to a prime candidate for an immediate, safe pilot program. This quantitative approach removes the guesswork from automation planning.

Readiness categories

The scorecard evaluates 10 critical areas: Workflow clarity, Process consistency, Data quality, Tool access, Ownership, Risk level, Human review plan, Customer experience impact, Measurement plan, and Rollback plan. Scoring high across these categories ensures that the automation will be robust, measurable, and recoverable if things go wrong.

Readiness bands

Based on the total score, workflows fall into four distinct bands. 0-10 (Danger): Do not automate yet; the process needs fundamental restructuring. 11-20 (Warning): Clean up first; address specific gaps in data or rules. 21-26 (Pilot): Proceed with a carefully monitored pilot. 27-30 (Ready): The workflow is primed for an automation rollout.

Example interpretation

Consider a 'Client Onboarding' workflow scoring 18/30. The scorecard might reveal high clarity and consistency, but poor data quality and missing human review plans. The interpretation is not 'build the agent,' but rather 'clean up the intake forms and define escalation rules before proceeding.' This saves time and prevents a disastrous customer experience.
